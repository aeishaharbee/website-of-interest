Power. Precision. Perseverance.

  • Ancient Origins

    The roots of Taekwondo can be found in ancient Korean martial arts such as Taekkyeon and Subak, which date back over 2,000 years.

  • Japanese Occupation (1910-1945)

    During this period, Korean martial arts were suppressed, and many Koreans were forced to learn Japanese martial arts like Karate.

  • Post-World War II

    After the end of World War II and the liberation of Korea from Japanese rule, there was a resurgence of interest in traditional Korean martial arts. Various martial arts schools and associations were formed.

  • Establishment of Taekwondo

    In the 1950s and 1960s, several Korean martial artists, including General Choi Hong Hi, played key roles in developing and promoting a unified form of Korean martial arts. In 1955, the name "Taekwondo" was officially adopted for this new martial art, with "Tae" meaning foot, "Kwon" meaning fist, and "Do" meaning way or path.

  • International Recognition

    Taekwondo gained international recognition in the 1960s and 1970s, with the establishment of various oranizations and federations promoting its practice worldwide.

  • Olympic Recognition

    One of the significant milestones in Taekwondo's history was its inclusion as an official Olympic sport. Taekwondo made its debut as a demonstration sport at the 1988 Seoul Olympics and became an official Olympic sport in 2000.

  • Modern Development

    Over the years, Taekwondo has evolved both as a sport and as a martial art. It emphasizes kicking techniques, particularly high and spinning kicks, along with punches, blocks, and stances. It places a strong emphasis on self-discipline, respect, and perseverance.

STANCES

Taekwondo employs various stances to facilitate movement, balance, and power generation. Common stances include the ready stance, horse stance, backstance, and fighting stance.

BLOCKS

Blocks are used to defend against incoming strikes. Common blocking techniques include the
rising block, low block, outer forearm block, and inner forearm block.

STRIKES

Taekwondo utilizes a variety of hand strikes, including punches and strikes with open hands. Basic hand strikes include the
jab, uppercut, backfist, and crosspunch.

KICKS

Kicking techniques are a hallmark of Taekwondo. They are characterized by speed, power, and precision. Basic kicks include the front kick, side kick, crescent kick, and roundhouse kick.

The white belt is the starting point for all Taekwondo practitioners. It symbolizes purity, innocence, and a beginner's mindset. White belt practitioners typically focus on learning basic techniques and etiquette.

Yellow belt signifies the earth from which a plant sprouts and takes root. At this stage, practitioners continue to build on their foundation, learning more advanced techniques and further developing their understanding of Taekwondo principles.

Green belt represents the growth and development of the plant as it reaches upward toward the sun. Practitioners at this level refine their techniques, increase their physical fitness, and deepen their understanding of Taekwondo philosophy.

Blue belt symbolizes the sky that the plant aspires to reach. At this stage, practitioners demonstrate increased proficiency in their techniques, focus on sparring skills, and begin to explore more complex combinations and strategies.

Red belt signifies danger and the need for caution as the practitioner approaches black belt level. At this advanced stage, practitioners hone their skills to a high level of precision, demonstrate leadership qualities, and prepare for the final test before obtaining a black belt.

The black belt represents the beginning of a new journey rather than the culmination of one. It signifies the attainment of a high level of skill, knowledge, and dedication to Taekwondo. Black belt practitioners continue to learn and grow, striving for mastery and contributing to the advancement of the art.

Poomsae are choreographed sequences of movements that simulate combat scenarios against imaginary opponents.

1

They emphasize the execution of techniques with precision, fluidity, and correct form.

2

Poomsae help practitioners develop muscle memory, balance, coordination, and focus.

3

Each poomsae has a specific sequence of movements and is performed with attention to rhythm, breathing, and concentration.

4

Poomsae serve as a way to express and refine the artistry of Taekwondo techniques.

5

Competitions may include demonstrations of poomsae, where practitioners are judged on factors such as accuracy, power, and presentation.
